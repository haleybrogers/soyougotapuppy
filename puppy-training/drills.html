Training drills

Quick, repeatable exercises. Do them daily.

Crate3
Crate reset
+
  1. Toss a treat into the crate — let them go in and come right back out
  2. Repeat 5–10 times with no door, no pressure
  3. You're rebuilding the association: crate = good things, zero strings attached

Use this when your puppy hesitates at the crate or has had a bad experience recently.

Build duration
+
  1. Puppy enters crate — close door for 5 seconds, then open
  2. Repeat, adding 5–10 seconds each time
  3. If they whine, wait for a pause in noise before opening
  4. Always open the door when they're calm, never when fussing

Don't rush. 30 calm seconds is better than 5 anxious minutes.

Calm entry reps
+
  1. Stand near the crate with a treat — wait for your puppy to offer going in
  2. Mark the moment they step inside — reward
  3. Release them, then wait again
  4. Repeat until they're choosing to go in without hesitation

You're teaching them the crate is their idea, not yours.

Potty2
Schedule reset
+
  1. Write down every time your puppy goes — for 3 full days
  2. Mark whether it was inside or outside, and what happened right before
  3. Use the pattern to build a schedule: out after waking, eating, play, every 1–2 hrs

If accidents are increasing, the schedule is the first thing to fix.

Potty spot focus
+
  1. Take your puppy to the same spot every time
  2. Stand still — no walking, no talking, no exploring
  3. Give them 3–5 minutes to sniff and go
  4. The moment they go → reward immediately at the spot

Wandering around the yard is the #1 reason potty trips take forever.

Calm4
Place reps
+
  1. Guide your puppy to their place (mat, bed, or towel)
  2. Reward the moment they're on it — even if they're standing
  3. Build toward lying down, then staying for 10, 20, 30 seconds
  4. Release with a word like "free" — then repeat

Short successful reps > one long stressful one. Build slowly.

Capturing calm
+
  1. Carry kibble around the house
  2. When your puppy lies down on their own → quietly toss a treat
  3. When they sit calmly or make soft eye contact → treat
  4. Don't say anything. Just reward calm whenever you see it

The most powerful drill that doesn't look like training.

Duration building
+
  1. Once your puppy holds place for 10 seconds reliably, start spacing rewards
  2. Reward at 10 sec, then 20, then 30 — randomize slightly
  3. If they break, reset calmly and start from a shorter interval
  4. Add mild distractions only after duration is solid

Randomize reward timing so they don't learn to predict it and bail early.

On/off switch
+
  1. Get a toy your puppy loves. Hold it behind your back
  2. Say "READY?" in an excited voice — then immediately play. Tug, chase, wrestling, whatever gets them fired up
  3. After 10–15 seconds of play, say "ENOUGH" in a calm, flat tone. Stop all movement. Toy goes behind your back. Stand still
  4. Wait for your puppy to settle — even a few seconds of stillness counts. The moment they calm down, mark it ("yes!") and the reward is saying "READY?" again — re-engaging in play is the reward. No food here.
  5. Do 3–5 rounds. Sometimes you just say "ENOUGH" and walk away — game over. They need to learn that too.
  6. Always end on a calm rep, not a play rep

This is one of the most powerful drills you can do. You're teaching your puppy that you control when the fun starts AND when it stops — and that coming down from excitement is just as rewarding as the excitement itself. The reward isn't food — it's getting to play again. "READY?" means go time. "ENOUGH" means it's over. They learn to switch states on your terms.

Recall3
Name game
+
  1. Toss a piece of kibble away from you — let them go get it
  2. While they're still eating (or just about to finish), say their name once — don't repeat it
  3. The instant they turn and run back to you → mark ("yes!") + reward with another piece of kibble
  4. Toss the next piece away and repeat. Over and over. You want: name → sprint to you → reward
  5. Do 10–15 reps. Try different rooms and different distances as they get it

Their name should mean "drop everything and run to mom/dad — something good is coming." Say it before they've finished eating so they're already in motion. Never use their name to scold. Every ignored rep weakens the cue, so only say it when they're likely to respond. Bonus: as they get better at this, start requiring more before the reward. Say their name + "COME", and wait for a sit before you give the kibble. Raise the bar gradually.

Short recall reps
+
  1. Stand 5–10 feet from your puppy
  2. Say "COME" in an upbeat tone
  3. When they arrive, reward heavily — treat party
  4. Let them wander, then repeat

Keep distance short so they always succeed. If you call and they don't come, you were too far or there was too much going on. Don't repeat the cue — go get them, and next time make it easier. Every ignored recall teaches them "come" is optional.

Distraction ladder
+
  1. Start with recall in a quiet room (Level 1)
  2. Move to a different room, then the backyard (Level 2)
  3. Add a mild distraction — toy on the ground, another person (Level 3)
  4. Take it outside on a long leash (Level 4)
  5. Only move up when they're 8/10 reliable at the current level
  6. Use a long line outside so you never have to give a recall you can't enforce

If they fail, you moved up too fast. Drop down and rebuild. The long line is your best friend — it means you never have to shout a command into the void and pray. Set them up so the right answer is easy.

Biting3
Redirect reps
+
  1. Keep a toy within reach at all times
  2. The moment teeth touch skin, interrupt with "ah-ah" and present the toy
  3. When they take the toy → praise and engage in play with it
  4. Repeat every single time — consistency is everything

Speed matters. The faster you redirect, the clearer the lesson.

Reverse timeout
+
  1. If biting continues after a redirect — stand up and turn away
  2. Cross your arms, no eye contact, no talking
  3. Wait 15–30 seconds, then re-engage calmly
  4. If they bite again immediately → you leave the room for 30 seconds

You're not punishing — you're teaching that biting makes the fun person disappear.

Engagement reset
+
  1. If your puppy is in full shark mode, stop all interaction
  2. Guide them to the crate or a calm area with a chew
  3. Let them decompress for 5–10 minutes
  4. Re-engage only when they've settled

Most biting spirals happen when the puppy is overtired. A reset is a kindness, not a punishment.

Manners3
Door wait reps
+
  1. Approach the door — ask for a sit or just wait for stillness
  2. Start opening the door slowly
  3. If they rush forward → close the door, reset
  4. If they stay → treat, then say "LET'S GO"
  5. Walk through together

The door is the test. Calm = it opens. Chaos = it closes. But if your puppy is already totally wired, don't even start this drill — manage with a leash and come back to it when they're in a state where they can actually think.

Threshold training
+
  1. Practice at any boundary — room to room, inside to outside, car door
  2. Pause before crossing. Reward the pause
  3. Release with a consistent word
  4. Repeat at 3–4 different thresholds throughout the day

You're teaching a pattern: movement happens with permission, not impulse.

Leash pressure intro
+
  1. Clip the leash on inside the house — no destination
  2. Apply light pressure in one direction
  3. The moment your puppy yields toward you → mark + reward
  4. Repeat in different directions — keep it short and low-pressure

This teaches them to follow leash guidance instead of fighting it. Start before you need it outside.

Socializing3
Distance exposure
+
  1. Find a new stimulus — a person, dog, bike, sound — at a comfortable distance
  2. Reward your puppy for noticing it calmly (look, then look back at you)
  3. If they react, increase distance until they can observe without tension
  4. Gradually decrease distance over multiple sessions

Distance is your best tool. If they can't be calm, you're too close.

Neutral observation
+
  1. Sit somewhere with mild activity — a bench, a patio, a quiet park
  2. Let your puppy watch people, dogs, or movement go by
  3. Reward any calm behavior — sitting, lying down, soft eyes
  4. Keep sessions short (5–10 min) and end before they get overwhelmed

They don't need to interact. Watching calmly is already a win.

Recovery reps
+
  1. After a mild startle or new experience, give your puppy space
  2. Don't comfort excessively — just be calm and present
  3. Wait for them to reset on their own (shake off, look at you, relax)
  4. Reward the recovery — that's the behavior you're building

How fast they bounce back matters more than whether they reacted.

Travel3
Carrier comfort
+
  1. Leave the carrier open at home with treats and a blanket inside
  2. Let your puppy explore it freely — no closing, no pressure
  3. Feed meals in the carrier once they're entering willingly
  4. Gradually close the door for short periods while they eat or chew

Treat the carrier exactly like crate training. Same rules, smaller space.

Movement simulation
+
  1. With your puppy settled in the carrier, pick it up and walk around
  2. Start with 30 seconds — set it down, reward calm
  3. Gradually increase duration and add new locations (car, lobby, sidewalk)
  4. Play ambient noise (airport sounds, engine noise) at low volume during sessions

You're teaching them that movement is boring. That's the goal.

Duration build
+
  1. Once carrier + movement feel normal, start extending time inside
  2. 15 min → 30 min → 1 hr — always with a chew or Kong
  3. Practice during calm periods, not when they're already wired
  4. Simulate real travel: carrier under a table at a cafe, in the car for errands

If you can get 2 calm hours in a carrier at home, the flight will be fine.

Meeting people3
Calm greeting setup
+
  1. Text your friend before they arrive: "Ignore the puppy completely until I say it's ok"
  2. When they enter, they keep hands to themselves — no eye contact, no baby voice
  3. Wait for your puppy to stop jumping, spinning, or whining
  4. The second your puppy is calm (even briefly) — mark it and let your friend say hello
  5. If the puppy escalates again, your friend goes back to ignoring

Coach the humans first. Most greeting chaos is a people problem, not a dog problem.

Door knock protocol
+
  1. Have someone knock or ring the doorbell
  2. Do NOT open the door yet — wait for your puppy to notice and react
  3. Ask for a sit or just wait for any calm behavior
  4. Reward the calm, then open the door slowly
  5. If they lose it again, close the door and reset
  6. Repeat until the knock doesn't trigger a meltdown

The doorbell is the trigger. You're teaching them that the sound means "settle," not "go crazy."

Stranger approach
+
  1. Have a friend your puppy hasn't met approach from a distance
  2. If your puppy pulls, jumps, or loses focus — stop and wait
  3. The friend only moves closer when your puppy is calm
  4. If your puppy can't settle, increase distance and start again
  5. Greeting only happens when all four paws are on the ground

You don't owe strangers access to your dog. "We're training" is a complete sentence.

Meeting dogs11

Meeting dogs — in your home

Scent swap
+
  1. Rub a blanket or towel on your existing dog, then place it near the puppy's crate or bed
  2. Do the same in reverse — give the older dog something with the puppy's scent
  3. Watch both dogs' reactions. Curiosity is good. Stiffening or avoidance means slow down.
  4. Repeat daily before the first face-to-face meeting
  5. You're building familiarity before the pressure of a real introduction

Do this for at least 2-3 days before any in-person introduction. Scent is how dogs get to know each other first.

Gate intro
+
  1. Set up a baby gate or barrier between the two dogs
  2. Let them see and sniff each other through the gate — no direct contact
  3. Reward both dogs for calm behavior (soft body, loose tail, no barking)
  4. If either dog stiffens, barks, or lunges — increase distance and try again later
  5. Keep sessions short: 2-3 minutes, then separate
  6. Gradually increase gate time over several days before removing the barrier

The gate lets both dogs opt out. That sense of control reduces stress for everyone.

Supervised coexist
+
  1. Both dogs in the same room — puppy on leash or tethered, older dog free to move
  2. Give each dog their own chew or activity so they're focused on something, not each other
  3. Reward both dogs periodically for ignoring each other — that's the behavior you want
  4. If the puppy fixates on or pesters the older dog, redirect with a toy or remove the puppy briefly
  5. End the session before either dog gets stressed — 10-15 minutes max early on
  6. Always give the older dog the option to leave the room

Coexistence is the goal, not play. If they can be in the same room doing their own thing, that's a win.

Meeting dogs — on the street

Watch and dismiss
+
  1. Find a spot where dogs pass at a comfortable distance — a park bench, sidewalk corner
  2. When your puppy notices a dog, let them look briefly
  3. The moment they look back at you (or you call their name and they respond) — reward heavily
  4. Repeat: notice dog → check in with you → reward
  5. If they can't look away, you're too close — move back

You're building the reflex: "I see a dog, I look at my person." That's the foundation of neutral behavior.

Street pass
+
  1. Walk your puppy on a normal route where you'll encounter other dogs
  2. When you spot a dog ahead, create space — cross the street or step to the side
  3. Keep walking at a steady pace. Don't stop, don't tighten the leash, don't change your energy
  4. Reward your puppy for continuing to walk with you past the other dog
  5. If your puppy reacts (pulling, barking, fixating) — stop, wait for calm, then keep moving
  6. The goal is passing another dog without it being an event

Your energy matters. If you tense up when you see another dog, your puppy feels it. Stay boring.

Body block
+
  1. Practice this at home first — have someone approach while you step between them and your puppy
  2. Turn your body sideways so you're a wall between the approaching person/dog and your puppy
  3. Stay calm. No yelling, no panic. Just calmly occupy the space
  4. Say "we're training" or "give us space" — keep it simple
  5. Once the person/dog passes, reward your puppy for staying behind you
  6. On walks, use this anytime an off-leash dog or pushy stranger approaches

This isn't aggressive — it's advocacy. You're your puppy's only line of defense out there.

Meeting dogs — off-leash and trails

Loose leash greeting
+
  1. When a greeting is happening — planned or not — immediately drop tension on the leash
  2. Don't reel your puppy in or hold them tight. A tight leash makes everything worse
  3. Stand still, breathe, keep your body relaxed. Your puppy mirrors your energy
  4. Let the dogs sniff briefly — 3-5 seconds is plenty
  5. If things are calm, you can let it continue. If either dog stiffens or escalates, calmly walk away
  6. Don't issue commands during the greeting — no "sit," no "leave it." Just be present

The leash is for safety, not control. If you're white-knuckling it, your puppy knows — and now they're stressed too.

Walk away reset
+
  1. After an unexpected or intense greeting, don't stop to debrief. Just walk
  2. Keep a steady, calm pace — no rushing, no stopping to comfort
  3. Let your puppy move and sniff for a few minutes. Movement helps them process
  4. After 2-3 minutes, check their body language: loose and happy = they're fine. Tucked and tense = they need more time
  5. Don't immediately try another dog interaction. Give them space to reset
  6. If they bounce back quickly, keep walking normally. If they're still stressed, head somewhere quiet

Not every encounter needs a lesson. Sometimes you just move on and let your puppy process it in their own time.

Meeting dogs — controlled intros

Parallel walk
+
  1. Find a friend with a calm dog — walk in the same direction with 10-15 feet between you
  2. Reward your puppy for walking without fixating on the other dog
  3. Gradually decrease distance over multiple sessions
  4. If your puppy lunges or can't focus, increase distance and try again
  5. The goal is neutral coexistence, not face-to-face play

Parallel walks teach your puppy that other dogs exist and it's not a big deal. That's the foundation for everything else.

Controlled intro
+
  1. Only attempt this with a known, calm dog — not random dogs on the street
  2. Start with parallel walking first (see Parallel walk drill)
  3. When both dogs are calm, allow a brief 3-second sniff on loose leashes
  4. Gently guide your puppy away with the leash and reward them for disengaging — don't use a recall here unless you're sure they'll respond. If they won't come, just walk away and let the leash do the work
  5. If either dog tenses up, stiffens, or fixates — create space immediately
  6. Keep it short. End on a win, not when things fall apart.

If the older dog growls, that's communication — don't punish it. Give both dogs space.

Decompression check
+
  1. After any dog-to-dog interaction, separate and give your puppy space
  2. Watch their body language for 5-10 minutes: are they shaking? Tucking their tail? Hiding?
  3. If they bounce back quickly (loose body, playful, relaxed) — the interaction was fine
  4. If they're shut down, clingy, or avoiding — that was too much. Note what went wrong
  5. Offer a calm activity like a chew or sniff walk to help them reset
  6. Use what you learned to adjust distance and duration next time

Recovery tells you more than the interaction itself. Always check how your puppy processes what just happened.

Handling7

Handling — paw and nail work

Paw touch reps
+
  1. Wait until your puppy is relaxed — after play or during a chew
  2. Rest your hand on their paw. Don't squeeze, don't hold. Just touch
  3. If they stay relaxed → treat. If they pull away → let them, and try lighter next time
  4. Gradually build: hold paw for 1 second → treat. 2 seconds → treat. Spread toes → treat
  5. Do this across multiple days. No rushing. Each step should feel like nothing to them

The goal is boredom, not tolerance. If they barely notice you're touching their paw, you're doing it right. If they're pulling away, you moved too fast — back up and make more deposits.

Clipper intro
+
  1. Set the clipper on the ground near your puppy. Let them sniff it → treat
  2. Pick it up and hold it near (not on) their paw → treat
  3. Touch the clipper to a nail without clipping → treat
  4. Clip one single nail → huge treat party. Done for the day
  5. Tomorrow, try two nails. Build to a full paw over a week or more
  6. If they pull away at any step, go back to the previous step and stay there longer

One nail is a win. Seriously. A dog who calmly lets you clip one nail will eventually let you clip all of them. A dog you wrestled through all four paws will fight you harder next time. Slow is fast.

Handling — brushing and grooming

Brush intro
+
  1. Let the brush sit on the ground near your puppy — treat for ignoring or sniffing it
  2. Pick it up, hold it near their shoulder — treat
  3. One gentle stroke on their back or shoulder → treat
  4. Two strokes → treat. Three → treat. Build gradually
  5. Stick to easy zones first (back, shoulders). Avoid legs, belly, and face until the easy spots are boring
  6. Pair with a lick mat or chew for longer sessions once they're comfortable

If your puppy bites at the brush, you skipped a step. Back up to just holding it near them. Make it boring before you make it functional. Set them up to win.

Sound desensitization
+
  1. Identify the sound: dryer, clippers, electric toothbrush — whatever they'll need to tolerate
  2. Play the sound from another room at low volume while your puppy eats or chews → treat
  3. Gradually move closer over multiple sessions — only advance if they stay relaxed
  4. Eventually run the tool in the same room, then near them, then briefly touching them
  5. If they startle, freeze, or try to leave — you jumped ahead. Go back two steps

This can take weeks for intense sounds like dryers. That's completely fine. A dog who doesn't panic at the groomer's dryer is worth every patient session. The piggy bank is full when they hear the sound and don't even look up.

Handling — vet and restraint prep

Mock exam
+
  1. With your puppy relaxed, gently look in one ear → treat
  2. Lift one lip to see their teeth → treat
  3. Touch their belly gently like you're examining → treat
  4. Gently move each leg through its range of motion → treat after each one
  5. Touch the base of their tail → treat
  6. Keep it short — 30 seconds to a minute. End while it's still easy

You're rehearsing everything the vet will do. If your puppy has done this 50 times at home with treats, the real exam isn't a surprise — it's just another Tuesday.

Restraint reps
+
  1. Gently hold your puppy still with both hands — just 2 seconds → treat + release
  2. Build to 5 seconds, then 10. Always release before they start to struggle
  3. Practice on a raised surface with a non-slip mat (mimics the vet table)
  4. If they squirm, hold gently until they give you even one second of stillness → mark + treat + release
  5. Never release during active struggling — you'll teach them that fighting works. Wait for a pause, even a tiny one

This is cooperative care in action. You're not overpowering them — you're teaching them that being still is rewarding and that they'll always be released. The piggy bank stays full when they trust you'll let go.

Happy visit
+
  1. Drive to the vet's office — just the parking lot at first. Sit for 5 minutes, treat, leave
  2. Next time, walk into the lobby. Let staff say hello and give treats. Leave after 5 minutes
  3. If your puppy is relaxed, ask if they can hop on the scale or sniff an exam room — treat the whole time
  4. Do the same at the groomer: walk in, treats, smell the place, leave
  5. Repeat 2-3 times before any real appointment

Most vets and groomers love happy visits — just call ahead and ask. You're making massive deposits in the piggy bank. When the real appointment comes, your puppy already knows this place means treats, not trauma.

Your dog is not the problem. Your inconsistency is.
