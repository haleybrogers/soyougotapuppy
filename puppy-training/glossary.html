Glossary

Dog training has a lot of jargon. Here's what it actually means.

Capturing
"When your puppy accidentally does something good and you throw food at them like a crazy person."
Rewarding behaviors the dog offers naturally, without prompting. One of the most powerful training methods.
Marker / marker word
"The 'yes!' or click that tells your dog 'THAT. That exact thing you just did. That's why food is coming.'"
A sound (clicker or verbal "yes") that precisely marks the moment the dog did the right thing, followed by a reward.
Positive reinforcement
"Adding something good (food, play, attention) to make a behavior happen more. AKA: bribery that works."
The only scientifically backed training methodology. Adding a desirable stimulus to increase behavior frequency.
Threshold
"The invisible line between 'I see that thing and I'm cool' and 'I SEE THAT THING AND I AM NOT COOL.' Work below it."
The point at which a stimulus becomes too intense for the dog to focus or learn. Effective training happens below threshold.
Shaping
"Rewarding baby steps until your dog accidentally invents the behavior you wanted. It's like hot/cold but with kibble."
Reinforcing successive approximations toward a target behavior. The dog learns to problem-solve actively.
Luring
"Holding a treat in front of their nose and guiding them like a little food-motivated puppet."
Using a treat or toy to physically guide the dog into position. Fade the lure within 3-5 reps or they become dependent on it.
Impulse control
"Teaching your puppy that screaming, biting, and being pushy doesn't get them what they want. Basically... adulting."
The ability to resist an immediate desire in order to achieve a better outcome. A foundational life skill for dogs.
Desensitization
"Making scary stuff boring by introducing it reallyyy slowly while feeding them so much they forget to be scared."
Gradual, controlled exposure to a stimulus at sub-threshold intensity, paired with positive associations.
Counter-conditioning
"Changing your dog's emotional response from 'THAT IS SCARY' to 'oh that thing means cheese happens.' "
Pairing a stimulus that causes an undesired response with something the dog loves to change the emotional association.
Extinction burst
"When you stop rewarding a behavior and they go HARDER before they give up. Like pressing an elevator button 47 times when it doesn't come."
A temporary increase in frequency/intensity of a behavior when reinforcement is removed. It gets worse before it gets better.
Place / mat training
"Teaching them that this specific mat = chill zone. It's portable calm. Take it to restaurants, friends' houses, anywhere."
Training a dog to go to a designated spot and remain there calmly. Builds duration and distraction tolerance.
Generalization
"Your puppy knows 'sit' in the kitchen but acts like they've never heard the word at the park. They need to learn skills transfer to new places."
A behavior learned in one context being performed in new contexts. Requires practice in multiple environments.
Enrichment
"Stuff that makes your dog use their brain so they don't use their teeth on your couch. Puzzle toys, sniff walks, frozen Kongs."
Activities that stimulate the dog's mind and fulfill species-typical needs. Essential for preventing boredom-based behavior problems.
Management
"Setting up the environment so your puppy can't practice the behavior you don't want. Baby gates, crates, keeping shoes in closets. Prevention > correction."
Environmental modification to prevent unwanted behaviors. Easier than training and should be used alongside it.
Fear period
"A developmental window where your puppy suddenly decides that the trash can they've walked past 100 times is actually a serial killer. Don't force them through it."
Developmental stages (~8-10 weeks and ~6-14 months) where puppies are hypersensitive to negative experiences. Requires gentle handling.
Dominance theory
"That thing your uncle keeps bringing up at Thanksgiving. It's been debunked. Even the original researcher said he was wrong. Let it go."
An outdated framework based on flawed 1930s captive wolf studies. Debunked by the original researcher. Not supported by modern behavioral science.
