Before your puppy comes home

things to set up, read, and decide

↓

don't wing this — a little setup now saves you a lot of chaos later

1 Set your rules now +

Decide before they're cute and getting away with everything:

  • couch or no couch
  • jumping allowed or no
  • sleeping in bed or no

if it's "fine for now" — it won't be fine later

2 Make their world small +

You don't need freedom. You need control.

  • crate
  • small area (pen or gated space)
  • supervised or contained at all times

if you're not watching them → they're contained

3 Pick your potty plan +

Don't improvise this.

  • same spot
  • same phrase
  • reward immediately

inconsistent here = confusing later

4 Get the right stuff (not more stuff) +

You don't need everything. Just:

  • crate
  • leash + harness
  • a few good chew toys
  • treats / kibble
  • baby gate or pen

that's enough

5 Get everyone on the same page +

One person breaking the rules = chaos still works.

everyone does the same thing — or nothing sticks

your puppy is about to start learning how the world works

make sure you like the version they learn

They're here. Now what?

the first week game plan

↓

take a breath — you don't need to train everything right now

1 Chill. Seriously. +

Your puppy just left their litter, their mom, and everything they've ever known. They are overwhelmed. You are overwhelmed. That's normal.

The first few days are not about training. They're about letting your puppy decompress and start feeling safe. Don't invite everyone over to meet the puppy. Don't take them to the pet store. Don't start drilling commands. Just exist together.

Let them sniff their space. Let them figure out where the water is. Let them sleep — a lot. Puppies need 18-20 hours of sleep a day. If yours seems wired and chaotic, they're probably overtired, not under-exercised.

the best thing you can do on day one is be boring

2 Introduce the house slowly +

Don't give your puppy the full house. That's way too much space and way too many places to have accidents, chew things, and get overwhelmed.

Start with one room. Their crate, their water, their potty access — that's their world for now. Once they're solid in that space (going potty outside, settling in the crate, not destroying everything), you can expand. One room at a time. Earn the space.

Block off stairs, close doors, use baby gates. Every room they can access unsupervised is a room where something will go wrong.

small world = fewer mistakes = faster learning

3 Start the crate immediately +

Night one. Yes, really. Your puppy sleeps in the crate from the very first night.

Will they cry? Probably. That's ok. Put the crate near your bed so they can hear and smell you. Toss a treat in, let them settle. If they whine, wait for a pause — even a two-second pause — then calmly reassure. Don't take them out when they're fussing or you'll teach them that fussing opens the door.

This is one of the most important things you'll do. A puppy who learns to sleep alone from the start is a puppy who doesn't develop separation anxiety. If you let them sleep in your bed "just for the first week," you're building a habit that gets exponentially harder to undo.

During the day, toss treats in the crate randomly. Feed meals in there. Make it the place where good things happen with zero pressure.

the crate isn't mean — it's the foundation of everything

4 What to actually train first +

Week one is not about sit, down, shake, or any tricks. It's about three things:

  • Name recognition — say their name, reward when they look. That's it. Do this 20 times a day.
  • Potty rhythm — out after sleeping, eating, playing. Same spot. Reward immediately when they go outside.
  • Crate comfort — in and out freely, treats inside, meals inside, door closed for short periods.

That's your entire week one curriculum. If you try to do more, you'll overwhelm both of you. These three things set the foundation for literally everything else.

don't teach commands they aren't ready for — you're just teaching them to ignore you

5 How much is too much +

People overthink this part. Here's the line:

If your puppy is checking out, yawning, getting nippy, or can't focus — you're doing too much. Stop. Let them sleep. Try again later when they're fresh.

Training sessions for a young puppy should be 2-3 minutes. Seriously. Maybe 5 if they're really engaged. Multiple short sessions throughout the day beats one long one every time.

And don't stack new experiences. One new thing per day is plenty in week one. New house is already a huge experience. You don't also need to add new people, new dogs, new places, and new commands on top of that.

if you're exhausted, they're exhausted — everyone go take a nap

6 The first night survival guide +

This is the part no one warns you about. The first night can be rough. Here's what to expect and what to do:

  • They will probably cry. This is normal. They just lost their entire family. Put the crate next to your bed, drop your hand near the crate door so they can smell you. Don't take them out when they're crying — wait for quiet.
  • They will need to go potty in the middle of the night. Young puppies can't hold it all night. Set an alarm for halfway through. Take them out, keep it boring (no play, no talking, just potty), then right back in the crate.
  • You will feel guilty. You're not hurting them. You're teaching them that nighttime is for sleeping and that being alone is safe. This is a gift you're giving them.
  • It gets better fast. Most puppies settle into the crate within 3-5 nights if you're consistent. If you cave and bring them to bed on night two, you reset the clock.

survive the first three nights and the rest gets dramatically easier

7 When to start "real" training +

You're already training. From the moment they walk in the door, your puppy is learning what works and what doesn't. Every interaction is data.

But structured drills and commands? Give it a few days. Once your puppy is eating normally, sleeping in the crate, and starting to figure out the potty rhythm — usually by the end of week one — you can start layering in short training sessions.

Start with the name game and capturing calm. Those don't require anything from your puppy except existing. Then add recall games inside the house. Then leash work. Build it up piece by piece.

The number one mistake people make is trying to do too much too soon. Your puppy just got here. Let them land. Then start teaching — slowly, in environments where they can succeed.

set them up to win from day one — even if "winning" is just sleeping in the crate

the first week feels like chaos — that's normal

your only job is structure, sleep, and not teaching bad habits

The puppy brain, explained

understand this and everything else clicks

↓

the rule

behavior → outcome → repeat (or don't)
jumping gets attention → jumping increases
sitting gets a treat → sitting increases
barking opens the crate → barking increases

the catch

your puppy decides what's rewarding — not you. eye contact, touch, freedom, even yelling "no" can be a reward if it got them engagement.

if it worked for them, it'll happen again

the shift

instead of:

"how do I stop this?"

→

ask:

"what is this getting them?"

remove the reward from what you don't want. add reward to what you do.

what works repeats — so choose carefully

the science behind this

add something good → behavior increases

this is what you'll use most

remove something good → behavior decreases

"bite = fun ends"

add something unpleasant → behavior decreases

we're not doing this here

remove something unpleasant → behavior increases

The puppy developmental timeline

what's normal at every age — your dog is taking notes, even if you're not

→ → → →

Baby Baby

the cute-but-clueless phase

EVERYTHING IS NEW. Peak socialization mode — their brain is a sponge. First fear period around 8-10 weeks. A single scary experience can have lasting effects.

Focus on

  • Socialization (people, sounds, surfaces)
  • Crate training + potty schedule
  • Name recognition
  • Hand feeding + recall game
  • Bite inhibition (soft mouth)
  • Handling (paws, ears, mouth + treats)

Don't freak out about

  • Potty accidents (they can't hold it)
  • Biting everything (mouth = hands)
  • 2-minute attention span

Common mistakes

  • Skipping socialization
  • Not enforcing naps
  • Punishing potty accidents

5 min walks per month of age. Let them sniff. No marathons.

Teething Gremlin

why is everything in their mouth

Baby teeth falling out, adult teeth coming in. Their mouth hurts. Everything must be chewed.

Focus on

  • LOTS of chew options (frozen Kongs, bully sticks)
  • Continue socialization (window closing at ~16 wks)
  • Basic obedience: sit, down, stay
  • Start leave it + drop it
  • Loose leash walking practice

Don't freak out about

  • Biting getting worse (teething pain)
  • Finding tiny teeth on the floor
  • Chewing intensity peaking

Common mistakes

  • Thinking socialization window is "later"
  • Not enough chew options
  • Too many commands at once

Teenage Dirtbag

they know what you want and are choosing not to do it

Independence increasing. They're testing boundaries. Like a tween who just discovered free will.

Focus on

  • Consistency — they're testing if rules are real
  • Recall on a long line (15-30 ft)
  • Impulse control drills
  • On/off switch practice
  • Place/mat training

Don't freak out about

  • Selective hearing (choosing, not forgetting)
  • Peak puppy energy
  • Regression on learned behaviors

Common mistakes

  • Giving up on recall
  • Off-leash before recall is solid
  • Not enough mental stimulation

Full Adolescent Chaos

most dogs get surrendered here. don't give up.

Brain is literally rewiring. Sexual maturity. Possible second fear period (6-14 months). Everything feels inconsistent.

Focus on

  • PATIENCE. This is the hardest part.
  • Continue all training — don't assume they "know it"
  • Increase mental stimulation
  • Practice in new environments
  • Stay calm during second fear period

Real talk

They haven't "forgotten" everything. Their brain is reorganizing. Like a teenager who suddenly can't do dishes even though they've done them 100 times. Keep showing up. It clicks eventually.

Finally a Dog

you survived. they survived. everyone survived.

Behavioral stabilization. Full emotional maturity (up to 2-3 years for large breeds). The foundation you built is paying off.

Keep doing

  • Training is ongoing. Not a phase. A lifestyle.
  • Mental enrichment daily
  • Recall practice (always worth it)
  • New experiences + socialization

You did it

If you've been consistent through the chaos, you now have a dog who understands how the world works. They know what gets them what they want. And hopefully... it's calm.
